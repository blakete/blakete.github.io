Most people try to optimize by working harder. More effort, more results. But the physics of driven systems suggests a different priority: phase alignment and low dissipation dominate raw effort. To see why, consider the simplest case.

A driven damped oscillator has three knobs you can turn. The first is F-naught, the driving force amplitude, that is, how hard you push, measured in newtons. The second is phi, the phase angle between the driving force and the system's motion, measured in radians. The third is gamma, the damping coefficient, that is, how much energy the system dissipates, measured in inverse seconds.

The steady-state energy stored in the system is given by: E equals F-naught squared times cosine squared of phi, divided by eight times gamma squared times m times omega-naught squared. That's the mass of the oscillator and its natural frequency in the denominator. If you analyze that formula carefully, it tells you everything!

Phase alignment is a gate. Cosine squared of phi ranges from one to zero. Push in sync with the system and you get full energy transfer. But push at phi equals pi over two, ninety degrees out of phase, and you transfer nothing, regardless of how large the force is.

Damping is an amplifier. Energy scales as one over gamma squared. Halve the dissipation and you store four times the energy. As damping approaches zero, stored energy diverges toward infinity. This is why resonance disasters happen: bridges collapse, wine glasses shatter. Tiny forces, negligible dissipation, and pushes that remain closely in phase.

Driving amplitude is quadratic leverage. Energy scales as F-naught squared. It matters, but it can't compensate for poor phase alignment. At phi equals pi over two, no amount of force transfers energy.

The formula confirms what's counterintuitive. Effort is quadratic, but phase is the only parameter that can zero out transfer completely. And low damping compounds small inputs into large stored energy.

The analogy extends beyond oscillators. Most people try to optimize by working harder: more amplitude. However, the highest-leverage moves are finding systems where your effort is naturally in phase with how the system wants to move, and reducing the dissipation that bleeds your invested energy away. Then you turn up the effort.
